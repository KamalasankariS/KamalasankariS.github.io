OFF THE CLOCK

The person and the stories Behind The Scenes.

PANEL 01

AI RESEARCHER

My research sits at the intersection of reasoning, explainability, and software security, places where AI is most often asked to be trustworthy and most often isn't. I work on structured attribution in small language models, stability of post-hoc explanations, and agentic systems that detect and repair vulnerabilities in code. I'm drawn to questions where the easy answer is suspicious: a model that scores well but reasons incoherently, an explanation that doesn't survive a small perturbation. The goal is always the same: systems that hold up under scrutiny, not just under benchmarks.

PANEL 02

BEST PAPER AWARDEE

My first published paper, YOLO: Roof Material Detection Using Aerial Imagery, won Best Paper at IEEE ICCDS 2024. It was a deep learning system for classifying roof materials from aerial imagery, aimed at urban analytics and disaster preparedness, and it improved mAP by 32.5% over YOLOv5. The award was generous, but what stayed with me was the problem: that careful work on something concrete, like helping a city understand its infrastructure before a storm hits, matters more than work that's technically flashier but disconnected from anyone's life. It set a standard I've kept since: pick problems where the answer would actually be useful to someone.

PANEL 03

PEER REVIEWER

I review for the ACL 2026 SURGeLLM Workshop. Reading other people's drafts carefully, finding what's supported by the evidence, what's quietly assumed, what claims do more work than the experiments behind them, has changed how I write more than any amount of self-editing. It's also taught me a kind of generosity: the reviewer's job isn't to win, it's to help the work get better. I try to write reviews I'd want to receive.

PANEL 04

TRAINED CARNATIC SINGER

More than ten years of Carnatic training rewired how I think. The art demands precision and a process-oriented mindset over quick results; the smallest shift in pitch matters, and you learn to hear structure everywhere. Years of repetition turned pattern recognition into instinct: calm, detail-oriented, self-correcting. It taught me patience with slow mastery and an ear for subtle inconsistencies, skills that carry far beyond music into how I approach research, code, and every problem worth solving.

PANEL 05

MENTORING

As a Student Academic Assistant at GW, I assisted professors in the Advanced Machine Learning course, mentoring students through assignments, debugging sessions, and project milestones, turning half-formed intuitions into working models and clean, defensible results. Outside the university, I also mentor students on independent projects and early-stage ideas, helping them navigate everything from scoping a problem to shipping something real. Teaching became one of the most rewarding parts of my work, and giving back to the community that shaped me is something I'll never stop doing.
